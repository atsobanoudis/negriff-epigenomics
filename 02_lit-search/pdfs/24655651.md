# Child Abuse, Depression, and Methylation in Genes Involved with Stress, Neural Plasticity, and Brain Circuitry

**Reference:** Weder, N., Zhang, H., Jensen, K., Yang, B. Z., Simen, A., Jackowski, A., Lipschitz, D., Douglas-Palumberi, H., Ge, M., Perepletchikova, F., O’Laughlin, K., Hudziak, J. J., Gelernter, J., & Kaufman, J. (2014). Child Abuse, Depression, and Methylation in Genes Involved with Stress, Neural Plasticity, and Brain Circuitry. *Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, 53(4), 417-424.e5. [PMID: 24655651]

---

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 190 children (94 maltreated and 96 healthy controls). Children ranged in age from 5 to 14 years, with a mean age of 10.2. The sample was 42% male and consisted of mixed racial backgrounds (17% European-American, 38% Hispanic, 30% African-American, and 15% biracial).
* **Sample Type:** Saliva-derived DNA.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina HumanMethylation450 (450K) BeadChip.
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Child maltreatment (abuse and/or neglect) and dimensional ratings of depression (Mood and Feelings Questionnaire - MFQ).
* **Methodological Summary:** Data were analyzed using Linear Mixed Effects (LME) models to account for familial correlations (siblings). Covariates included age, sex, and race. Significance was defined by a genome-wide threshold of p < 5.0e-07. Generalized estimating equations (GEE) were used for combined models of maltreatment and methylation.

## Summary Notes

* **Functional Pathways:** The study identified significant epigenetic markers in genes critical to the biological response to stress (ID3), neural plasticity (GRIN1), and the development/maintenance of neural circuitry and white matter (TPPP).
* **Independent Risk Factors:** Methylation changes in ID3, TPPP, and GRIN1 were found to be independent predictors of depression symptoms, providing risk above and beyond the experience of maltreatment itself.
* **Reversibility and Intervention:** The authors emphasize that while epigenetic changes associated with early adversity are long-lasting, they are not permanent. Interventions focusing on secure attachment, environmental enrichment, and clinical treatment for psychopathology may help reverse negative biological sequelae.
* **Study Limitations:** The findings are limited by a modest sample size, the use of peripheral (saliva) DNA rather than brain tissue, and the lack of concurrent gene expression data to confirm the functional impact of the methylation.
