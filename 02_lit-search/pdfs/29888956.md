# Sex-specific epigenetic mediators between early life social disadvantage and adulthood BMI

**Reference:** Chu, S. H., Loucks, E. B., Kelsey, K. T., Gilman, S. E., Agha, G., Eaton, C. B., Buka, S. L., & Huang, Y. T. (2018). Sex-specific epigenetic mediators between early life social disadvantage and adulthood BMI. *Epigenomics*, 10(6), 707-722. [PMID: 29888956]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The final analytic sample consisted of 143 participants (69 males, 74 females) from the Longitudinal Effects on Aging Perinatal (LEAP) Project, a substudy of the New England Family Study. The mean age was 46.9 years. The sample was 66.4% white and 33.6% non-white.
* **Sample Type:** DNA was extracted from subcutaneous adipose tissue (aspirated from the buttock) and peripheral blood (buffy coat).
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina Infinium HumanMethylation450 BeadChip array.
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Early Life Social Disadvantage (ELSD), measured at age 7 using a composite summary score of ten components: parental income, parental occupation, household crowding, family structure, changes in marital status, number of moves, parental employment history, changes in primary caregiver, death of a sibling, and change in economic situation.
* **Methodological Summary:** The study used a sex-specific two-stage epigenome-wide mediation analysis.
* **Stage 1:** Association between CpG methylation and adult BMI (p < 0.005).
* **Stage 2:** Association between CpG methylation and ELSD (p < 0.005).
* **Covariates:** Age at assessment, race, sex, prenatal maternal smoking, and BMI at age 7. Blood analyses were additionally adjusted for cell mixture deconvolution.
* **Mediation Testing:** Cross-product-based mediation analysis was used to estimate Indirect Effects (IE) and Direct Effects (DE).

## Summary Notes

* **Sex and Tissue Specificity:** Significant mediation by DNA methylation was found only in adipose tissue, not in blood. The specific CpG sites acting as mediators were entirely different for males and females, suggesting sex-specific biological responses to early life adversity.
* **Enriched Biological Pathways:** In females, pathway analysis (GREAT) identified enrichment in immunologic signatures (macrophages, T cells), TGF-beta signaling, and MAPK signaling. In males, while fewer functional groupings were apparent, significant loci (MAP3K5, RPTOR) are linked to adipogenesis and insulin resistance.
* **Toxic Stress Hypothesis:** The findings support the "toxic stress" model, where social adversity in childhood may systemically modify the epigenetic landscape of adipose tissue, leading to dysregulation of inflammation and metabolism that manifests as higher adult BMI.
* **Study Limitations:** The authors noted they could not adjust for cell-type heterogeneity in adipose tissue due to sample size. Additionally, as a prospective observational study, they cannot definitively prove the direction of causality between methylation and BMI, though biochemical dynamics suggest methylation changes likely precede weight changes.
