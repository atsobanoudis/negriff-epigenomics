# DNA methylation differences associated with social anxiety disorder and early life adversity

**Reference:** Wiegand, A., Kreifelts, B., Munk, M. H. J., Geiselhart, N., Ramadori, K. E., Macisaac, J. L., Fallgatter, A. J., Kobor, M. S., & Nieratschker, V. (2021). DNA methylation differences associated with social anxiety disorder and early life adversity. *Translational Psychiatry*, 11, 104. [PMID: 33542190]

---

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 143 participants of Caucasian descent, aged 18-50 years. The cohort was divided into four groups:
* Control participants without SAD and low ELA (n = 47)
* Control participants without SAD and high ELA (n = 30)
* SAD participants with low ELA (n = 35)
* SAD participants with high ELA (n = 31)


* **Sample Type:** Whole blood.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina Infinium MethylationEPIC Kit (850K).
* **Primary Exposure(s):** 
* Diagnosis of Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) based on SCID (DSM-IV).
* Early Life Adversity (ELA) measured via the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ).


* **Methodological Summary:** 
* **Covariates:** Age, sex, and estimated blood cell type composition (monocytes, granulocytes, CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, B cells, and natural killer cells).
* **Thresholds:** For main effects (SAD and ELA), Differentially Methylated Regions (DMRs) required at least two CpG sites with FDR-corrected p-value < 0.05 and a mean DNA methylation difference > 5%. For interaction effects, DMRs required at least three CpG sites with FDR < 0.05 and a DNA methylation range > 5%.

## Summary Notes

* **Convergent Pathways in Anxiety:** The study identified SLC43A2 and TNXB as genes associated with Social Anxiety Disorder. Both genes have been previously linked to panic disorder, suggesting they may be involved in broader psychophysiological mechanisms underlying various anxiety disorders.
* **Specific Adversity Signatures:** Different types of ELA may leave distinct epigenetic marks. For example, SLC17A3 methylation was nominally associated with physical abuse, whereas SIAH3 methylation showed a significant negative correlation with emotional abuse and emotional neglect.
* **Interaction and Cumulative Effects:** The findings regarding C2CD2L suggest a cumulative effect of stress and anxiety; hypermethylation was only observed in individuals who suffered from both high ELA and current SAD. In contrast, MRPL28 showed increased methylation in control individuals with high ELA, which was not present in those with SAD, potentially indicating a "reversal" or different epigenetic trajectory in the presence of clinical anxiety.
* **Study Limitations:** The authors noted several caveats, including a relatively small sample size, the use of whole blood instead of brain tissue (which may not perfectly reflect central nervous system changes), and a lack of direct genotype data to fully control for mQTL effects. Additionally, while certain regions were associated with both ELA and SAD severity, they did not statistically mediate the relationship between ELA and adult SAD.
