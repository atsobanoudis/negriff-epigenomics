# Child Abuse and Epigenetic Mechanisms of Disease Risk

**Reference:** Yang, B. Z., Zhang, H., Ge, W., Weder, N., Douglas-Palumberi, H., Perepletchikova, F., Gelernter, J., & Kaufman, J. (2013). Child Abuse and Epigenetic Mechanisms of Disease Risk. *American Journal of Preventive Medicine*, 44(2), 101-107. [PMID: 23332324]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 192 children (96 maltreated and 96 demographically-matched controls). Participants ranged in age from 5 to 14 years (mean age 10.2). The sample was 42% male and of mixed racial origin (17% European-American, 38% Hispanic, 30% African-American, and 15% biracial).
* **Sample Type:** Saliva DNA specimens.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina HumanMethylation450 (450K) BeadChip.
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Child maltreatment, specifically children removed from parental care due to verified reports of abuse or neglect (including physical, sexual, and emotional abuse, as well as neglect and witnessing domestic violence).
* **Methodological Summary:** Data were analyzed using a linear mixed-effects model (LME) to account for familial correlations. Covariates included age, gender, and race. The significance threshold for genome-wide analyses was set to 5.0 x 10^-7 to correct for multiple comparisons.

## Summary Notes

* **Widespread Epigenetic Impact:** Child maltreatment is associated with widespread methylation differences across the entire genome (2,868 significant CpG sites). A distinct pattern was observed where maltreated children showed elevated methylation at low-to-mid range sites and reduced methylation at high-range sites.
* **Disease Pathway Enrichment:** Differentially methylated genes are significantly enriched in pathways related to various cancers (lung, colorectal, breast, prostate), psychiatric disorders (neurogenesis, axonal guidance), and metabolic/cardiovascular health (leptin signaling, cardiac development, and inflammation).
* **Study Limitations:** The authors note several caveats, including a modest sample size, the lack of longitudinal health data, the absence of gene expression data to confirm functional impact, and the use of peripheral (saliva) rather than tissue-specific DNA.
* **Potential for Reversibility:** While epigenetic changes associated with early adversity appear long-lasting and contribute to lifelong disease risk, the authors emphasize that these modifications are potentially reversible through pharmacological or environmental interventions.
