# War Exposure and DNA Methylation in Syrian Refugee Children and Adolescents

**Reference:** Smeeth, D., Ecker, S., Chervova, O., McEwen, F., Karam, E., Beck, S., & Pluess, M. (2025). War Exposure and DNA Methylation in Syrian Refugee Children and Adolescents. *JAMA Psychiatry*, 82(2), 191-200. [PMID: 39565630]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 1507 Syrian refugee children and adolescents. The mean age was 11.3 years (SD 2.4), with an age range of 6 to 19 years. The sample consisted of 793 females (52.6%) and 714 males (47.4%). Families were primarily Syrian (98.5%) and Sunni Muslim (96.9%).
* **Sample Type:** Saliva samples were used for DNA extraction and methylation profiling.
* **EWAS Platform:** Infinium Methylation EPIC v1.0 BeadChip (Illumina), profiling 865,859 sites.
* **Primary Exposure(s):** War exposure was assessed using the War Events Questionnaire (25-item checklist). Specific measures included Total War Exposure, Any War Exposure, Bombardment, Other-Directed Violence, Violence Directed Toward a Close Person, War-Related Violence in the Home, and Personal Harm.
* **Methodological Summary:** Analyses used probe-wise linear models (Limma) and linear mixed-effects models for longitudinal data. Covariates included age, sex, nationality, smoking status, time since leaving Syria, pubertal status, BMI, and current living environment quality. The significance threshold for DMPs was set at Bonferroni-corrected alpha = 0.05 (two-sided). DMRs were identified using DMRcate (Stouffer P < 0.05).

## Summary Notes

* **Enriched Biological Pathways:** Gene set enrichment analyses identified that genes associated with differential methylation were overrepresented in pathways involving transmembrane transport (specifically amino acid sodium symporter activity), neurotransmission (glutamate receptor activity), protein degradation (proteasome accessory complex), and intracellular movement (microfilament motor activity).
* **Epigenetic Aging Findings:** Contrary to the authors' initial hypotheses, war exposure (specifically bombardment and any war exposure) was nominally associated with *decreased* epigenetic aging according to the Horvath multitissue and Wu clocks. The authors suggest this might represent delayed physical or cognitive development in response to severe trauma.
* **Replication Failure:** The study failed to replicate 99.2% of previously reported trauma-associated DNA methylation sites. Only 2 out of 258 previously identified CpGs were significantly associated with war exposure in this cohort, highlighting potential differences between war-related trauma and other forms of childhood adversity, or population-specific genetic/environmental factors.
* **Study Limitations:** Key caveats include the lack of data on the timing or chronicity of war events, the use of heterogeneous saliva tissue, and a relatively small "unexposed" control group, as 97% of the refugee sample had experienced war events.
