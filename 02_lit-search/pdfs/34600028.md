# Differential regulation of the DNA methylome in adults born during the Great Chinese Famine in 1959-1961

**Reference:** Li, S., Wang, W., Zhang, D., Li, W., Lund, J., Kruse, T., Mengel-From, J., Christensen, K., & Tan, Q. (2021). Differential regulation of the DNA methylome in adults born during the Great Chinese Famine in 1959-1961. *Genomics*, 113(6), 3907-3918. [PMID: 34592358]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 184 participants from Qingdao, China. The exposed group consisted of 79 adults born during the famine (January 1959 – December 1961; mean age 57). The unexposed control group consisted of 105 adults born after the famine (January 1963 – May 1964; mean age 53).
* **Sample Type:** Whole blood.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina Infinium MethylationEPIC BeadChip (850K).
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Early-life exposure (prenatal and infancy) to the Great Chinese Famine (1959–1961).
* **Methodological Summary:** DNA methylation M-values were analyzed using linear regression, adjusting for age, sex, and estimated blood cell-type proportions (CD8T, CD4T, NK, B cells, monocytes, and granulocytes). Significant age-dependent sites were filtered out prior to analysis.
* **Significance Thresholds:** p < 1e-06 for suggestive significance; 1e-06 < p < 1e-05 for weaker-than-suggestive significance; False Discovery Rate (FDR) < 0.05 for genome-wide significance; FWER < 0.1 for Differentially Methylated Regions (DMRs).

## Summary Notes

* **Predominant Hypomethylation:** The study revealed a systematic pattern of decreased DNA methylation (hypomethylation) in adults exposed to famine during early life, particularly in the "open sea" and intergenic regions of the genome.
* **Enriched Functional Pathways:** Functional annotation and gene-set enrichment analysis (GSEA) identified that the differentially methylated sites were primarily involved in neurodevelopment (neurogenesis, neuron differentiation), neuropsychological disorders (schizophrenia), and metabolism.
* **Stress-Related Signatures:** The identification of DMRs in genes like *RNF39* and *PTPRN2*—which are also altered in conditions like post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR)—suggests that early-life famine creates a long-lasting "epigenetic embedding" of prenatal stress.
* **Caveats:** The authors noted that the control group was on average 4 years younger than the exposed group, though they implemented a specific filtering pipeline to remove age-associated CpG sites to mitigate this bias. Additionally, the study was observational and conducted in whole blood, which may not reflect tissue-specific changes in the brain.
