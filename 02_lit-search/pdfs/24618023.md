# Childhood abuse is associated with methylation of multiple loci in adult DNA

**Reference:** Suderman, M., Borghol, N., Pappas, J. J., Pinto Pereira, S. M., Pembrey, M., Hertzman, C., Power, C., & Szyf, M. (2014). Childhood abuse is associated with methylation of multiple loci in adult DNA. *BMC Medical Genomics*, 7:13. [PMID: 24618023]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** 40 adult white males from the 1958 British Birth Cohort (males born in a single week in March 1958). The core analysis compared 12 individuals who reported childhood abuse against 28 who did not. A replication study for specific loci was conducted in an additional 27 males.
* **Sample Type:** Peripheral blood cells (DNA extracted from whole blood collected at age 45).
* **EWAS Platform:** Methylated DNA Immunoprecipitation (MeDIP) followed by hybridization to custom-designed, high-density oligonucleotide microarrays. The arrays covered promoters of 20,533 genes and 489 microRNAs (approx. 1000 bp upstream to 250 bp downstream of transcription start sites).
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Childhood abuse occurring before age 16, identified via retrospective self-report at age 45. This included verbal abuse, humiliation/bullying, physical abuse, and sexual abuse.
* **Methodological Summary:**
* **Statistical Threshold for Promoters:** False Discovery Rate (FDR) < 20% (exploratory threshold).
* **Probe-level Significance:** Modified t-statistic p < 0.05 and log2 fold-difference >= 0.25.
* **Covariates:** The study accounted for childhood and adulthood socio-economic position (SEP).
* **Validation/Replication:** Quantitative PCR (qPCR) for 11 genes and bisulfite pyrosequencing for PM20D1 and SLC17A3.

## Summary Notes

* **Pathway Enrichment:** Differentially methylated genes were significantly enriched in key cell signaling pathways related to **transcriptional regulation** (chromatin and histone modification) and **development** (multicellular organismal development). Specifically, the **WNT signaling pathway** showed significant hypomethylation, suggesting potential activation of this pathway in the blood cells of the abuse group.
* **MicroRNA Coordination:** The study found a "double layer of repression" mechanism where hypermethylated microRNAs (which would typically repress target genes) were paired with **hypomethylated gene targets**, implying a systemic lifting of gene repression associated with childhood abuse.
* **Genomic Clustering:** Methylation changes were not randomly scattered but clustered in discrete genomic regions (megabase-sized) and across specific chromosomes (16, 17, and X), suggesting a large-scale, organized biological response to early-life adversity.
* **Study Caveats:** The authors noted several limitations, including the small sample size (n=40), the reliance on retrospective self-reporting of abuse, and the use of whole blood, which contains a mix of cell types that may fluctuate. They also emphasized that while the findings are robust, they demonstrate associations rather than causal links.
