# Differential DNA methylation in peripheral blood mononuclear cells in adolescents exposed to significant early but not later childhood adversity

**Reference:** Esposito, E. A., Jones, M. J., Doom, J. R., MacIsaac, J. L., Gunnar, M. R., & Kobor, M. S. (2016). Differential DNA methylation in peripheral blood mononuclear cells in adolescents exposed to significant early but not later childhood adversity. *Development and Psychopathology*, 28(4 Pt 2), 1385-1399. doi:10.1017/S0954579416000055. [PMID: 26847422]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 83 total participants: 50 adolescents adopted from Russian or Eastern European institutions (average age 15.68 years) and 33 non-adopted adolescents of European descent raised in their biological families in the United States (average age 15.41 years). Both groups were Caucasian and roughly matched on family income and parental education levels.
* **Sample Type:** Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) isolated from whole blood.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina Infinium HumanMethylation450 BeadChip.
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Early childhood adversity, specifically institutional (orphanage) rearing prior to international adoption, compared to non-adopted controls. Adoptees spent an average of 22 months in institutional care before being moved to well-resourced environments.
* **Methodological Summary:** The researchers controlled for age, sex, and negative life events. A critical correction for white blood cell (WBC) type composition was performed. The significance threshold for the filtered analysis was set at a q-value of 0.1 and a minimum mean difference in methylation of 2% (0.02 beta).

## Summary Notes

* **Enriched Functional Pathways:** Functional enrichment analysis identified that group differences in DNA methylation were clustered primarily in **neural** (e.g., behavior response, action potential, synapse) and **developmental** (e.g., organ induction, morphogenesis, nephron tubule development) categories.
* **Immune Competence:** The study found a "striking" difference in white blood cell composition, with adopted youth showing a pattern (lower CD4+/CD8+ ratio) suggestive of reduced immune competence compared to US-born peers. This pattern was consistent with other children born into poverty in the same region, rather than institutionalization alone.
* **Environmental Signatures:** Differential methylation in the **CYP1A1** and **AHRR** genes suggests that early life exposure to cigarette smoke in Eastern European institutions may have left a lasting epigenetic signature detectable years later.
* **Methodological Caveat:** The authors emphasize that failing to correct for cell-type composition would have led to a massive overestimation of significant results (136,339 sites vs. 30 sites), highlighting the necessity of accounting for cellular heterogeneity in peripheral blood EWAS.
