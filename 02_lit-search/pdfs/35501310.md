# An epigenetic association analysis of childhood trauma in psychosis reveals possible overlap with methylation changes associated with PTSD

**Reference:** Løkhammer, S., Stavrum, A. K., Polushina, T., Aas, M., Ottesen, A. A., Andreassen, O. A., Melle, I., & Le Hellard, S. (2022). An epigenetic association analysis of childhood trauma in psychosis reveals possible overlap with methylation changes associated with PTSD. *Translational Psychiatry*, 12(1), 177. [PMID: 35501310]

## Study Overview

* **Population & Demographics:** The study included 602 patients of European ancestry diagnosed with severe mental disorders (schizophrenia group: n=268; bipolar disorder group: n=229; other psychosis group: n=105). The age range was 18 to 64 years, with a median age of 28.0 years.
* **Sample Type:** Fasting peripheral blood samples.
* **EWAS Platform:** Illumina Infinium Methylation EPIC BeadChip (759,742 probes remaining after quality control).
* **Primary Exposure(s):** Childhood Trauma (CT) measured via the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire-short form (CTQ-SF), covering five subtypes: emotional abuse, physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional neglect, physical neglect, and a total trauma score.
* **Methodological Summary:** Linear regression was performed using "Model A," which included trauma score, age, sex, smoking, and the first five principal components as covariates. Significance thresholds were set at FDR < 0.05 for Differentially Methylated Positions (DMPs) and Šidák p < 0.05 for Differentially Methylated Regions (DMRs).

## Summary Notes

* **Functional Pathways:** While formal gene pathway analysis yielded no significant results due to the low number of genes, the authors identified individual genes (e.g., *TANGO6*, *ALOX12*, *PM20D1*, *CREB5*) involved in neural migration, oxidative stress, synaptic plasticity, and the cytoskeleton.
* **PTSD Overlap:** Several identified DMRs (specifically in *ALOX12* and *PM20D1*) overlap with methylation changes previously reported in PTSD populations, suggesting a shared biomolecular link between childhood trauma and stress-related psychopathology.
* **Tissue Limitations:** The authors noted that using blood as a surrogate for brain tissue may overlook disease-specific patterns, although they cited high blood-brain concordance (94%) for CpG-SNPs in schizophrenia patients.
* **Reversibility Caveat:** A major study limitation is the potential for DNA methylation changes to be reversed over time (e.g., through cell division/renewal of white blood cells), which may affect findings in an adult sample retrospectively reporting childhood trauma.
